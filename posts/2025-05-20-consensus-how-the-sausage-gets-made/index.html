Consensus: How the sausage gets made

What can we learn about the experts involved in the recent social media effects consensus preprint and what does that say about the consensus.

Ruben C. Arslan https://rubenarslan.github.io (Psychological Research Methods, University Witten/Herdecke)
2025-05-20

A large working group of psychologists recently put out a consensus statement on social media and mental health effects, based on Haidt’s book “The Anxious Generation”.

Many of the scholars I respect the most in this space (e.g., Lucy Foulkes, Amy Orben, Andrew Przybylski) seem to have not participated or dropped out of this effort. I know that in at least two cases this was because they did not trust the process. I am not an expert in this field myself, but I did read “The Anxious Generation”, “What Mental Illness Really Is … And What It Isn’t” and a few other things and have some familiarity with how strong the evidence base is. Now, personally, I think this consensus effort is premature. For many of the claims they examined we don’t have the evidence to assess many of these claims well. Consensus is not a shortcut to understanding the world.

In the end, the participants found consensus for only one causal claim (“Heavy smartphone and social media use can cause sleep problems.”), several weak claims about associations (e.g., “social media may be associated with visual social comparison in girls”) as well as a claim that adolescent mental health has declined in several Western countries. To me, not even the evidence for this mental health decline is very compelling. But generally this consensus is not far away from my own current understanding. What irks me is how this was communicated and what they chose to focus on.

It’s not your fault if recent social media posts led you to believe there was a “ton of consensus” that “Adolescent mental health has declined”1 or that rejecting this consensus is akin to denying climate change.2

Screenshot of now deleted tweet by Jay van Bavel

However, even if I don’t think the consensus statement has much value, I think it’s meta-scientifically interesting to look at how the participating scholars assessed the level of evidence for each claim. Maybe this will also give us an idea what kind of experts self-selected into the process. Potentially, the Delphi method corrected for all problems. I am not trying to assess that. I am only looking at Study 1 here to understand “how the sausage gets made”. Similarly, I found it interesting to read how the first author dealt with critical comments, documented on OSF.

Naively, you might expect that sometimes, when people have expertise in one area but not another, they would not answer questions in those areas where they cannot assess the evidence base. You would not expect them to report a stronger evidence base than exists. Consider replicated experimental field studies. These are very effortful studies, done either at considerable expense or making use of widely reported policy changes. You certainly wouldn’t expect a researcher to say such studies exist in their field of expertise when they don’t. In fact, I fail my students when they make claims their citations cannot support (I’ve gotten stricter about this because it’s such an LLM hallmark). WELL!

You might be surprised to hear that, initially, for at least seven claims a substantial fraction of scholars said there were causally informative field studies yet none were cited in the final consensus statement’s evidence summary according to language model Gemini 2.5 Pro.3 The first author, Valerio Capraro, seems to agree that the evidence for most claims is weak and correlational. So, I don’t think my assessment that some scholars overestimated the evidence is out of whack with the first author’s views.

So, who are these scholars who think there have been replicated causally informative field studies showing that social media affects body image? Maybe they have references that didn’t make the cut. Unfortunately, I don’t know! Turns out the responses were anonymized, as is standard in the Delphi method. I.e. even for those scholars who participated non-anonymously, we don’t know what they said for each claim. Expertise without accountability? Truly, you can have it all.

What question did they answer exactly when assessing the evidence base for causal claims?

To the best of your knowledge, what is the level of evidence regarding this claim?

  • ▢ No evidence, only opinions, perspectives, general theory or anecdotes (1)
  • ▢ Some correlational evidence (laboratories, surveys, online, field) (2)

As far as I could tell at a glance, the authors didn’t precisely define what counts as causal vs. correlational. I’m one of those weirdos who believe you can infer causality from observational/correlational data if you’re careful, but from the contrast to “correlational”, I guess the authors considered only experiments and natural experiments “causal evidence”.

Now, after examining this graph, you would be excused if you thought these categories were mutually exclusive. But no:

Moreover, after careful consideration within the core group, we decided to allow multiple answers for this question to avoid forcing participants to rank the level of evidence.

Yes, you certainly wouldn’t want to rank levels of evidence. How would those poor correlational studies and opinions feel? Still, you might be confused, after all, Figure 3 (above) tabulates them as if responses summed to 100%. But actually authors could and did endorse multiple mutually exclusive answers. Most obviously, more than 70 responses say both there is “no evidence” and at the same time say there is evidence. I guess they exist in a state of superposition. It would collapse if we actually observed the evidence, but, like, who has the time?

I looked into this because several experts claimed that there were replicated causally informative field studies showing that social media increases visual social comparison in girls. That seemed odd, because a) this seems difficult to study experimentally in the field b) I looked at this literature briefly a while ago and didn’t encounter anything of the sort. So, I checked the evidence summary in Supplementary Section S5. Indeed, no field studies are highlighted at all. Weird. Did they imagine these studies? Do they exist, but for some reason the best-quality evidence (sorry, sorry, I know evidence shan’t be ranked) isn’t mentioned?

To find out how this stacks up for the other claims, I let Gemini 2.5 Pro take a stab at tabulating how often experts say studies exist that then seem to be missing in action in the evidence review. I only hand-checked the visual social comparison section. Gemini agreed with my assessment there. So, here we have it: It’s not just LLMs that occasionally hallucinate scientific papers.

Does this mean the consensus statement is wrong? They went through three more rounds and had a chance to rectify such problems with grounding. Still, I worry that people who admit uncertainty self-selected out and people who incorrectly believe causally informative field studies exist where there are none, self-selected in.
